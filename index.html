Introduction

How has employment in the Philippines changed over the past two decades, and what do recent patterns reveal about who is actually working as the country moves into an AI-intensive future? This blog distills findings from an analysis of Philippine Statistics Authority (PSA) Labor Force Survey data spanning 2005 to early 2026.

The Philippines is a compelling case study for two reasons. First, it combines persistent structural challenges — high underemployment and wide gender gaps in labor force participation — with fast-growing service sectors like BPO that are directly exposed to AI-enabled automation. Second, evidence on how AI affects employment in middle-income economies remains scarce, making careful, descriptive baselines essential before drawing causal conclusions.

Key framing: The analysis uses three periods for comparison: 2005–2018 (pre-pandemic baseline), 2019–2021 (pandemic & early recovery), and 2022–2025 (post-pandemic / AI-era window). The 2022–2025 period coincides with the mainstream diffusion of generative AI — but this study treats that as a temporal reference, not a causal claim.

Long-Run Labor Market Trajectories (2005–2025)

Before zooming into the AI era, we need the full 20-year backdrop. Four indicators tell the story: labor force participation, employment, unemployment, and underemployment. Three events punctuate the timeline: the 2008–09 Global Financial Crisis, the 2020–21 COVID-19 shock, and the post-2022 generative-AI diffusion window.

Labor Force Participation Rate (LFPR)
Annual, Philippines 2005–2025 • Share of population aged 15+ in the labor force

A Stable Mid-60s Corridor

From 2005 to 2025, the LFPR fluctuates within a narrow band of roughly 60%–65%. COVID-19 produced only a modest dip. The 2022–2025 values sit squarely within the historical corridor, meaning AI-era employment changes unfold against a backdrop of long-standing, relatively flat participation — not a sudden surge or collapse in who is available to work.

Employment Rate
Annual, Philippines 2005–2025 • Employed persons as % of the labor force

Consistently High, COVID Dip Quickly Reversed

Employment rates remained in the low-to-mid 90s throughout. The COVID shock pushed it toward 90%, but by 2022–2025 it recovered to 95–96%, matching or slightly exceeding pre-pandemic levels. Headline employment in the Philippines looks at least as strong in the AI era as in much of the pre-COVID period.

Unemployment Rate
Annual, Philippines 2005–2025 • Unemployed persons as % of the labor force

A Gradual Long-Run Decline

Unemployment fell from ~8% in the mid-2000s to ~4–5% by 2025, punctuated by a COVID spike to ~10%. The post-2022 window continues a pre-existing downward trajectory. This is consistent with international evidence that generative AI has not yet produced large aggregate unemployment shocks — even if it may be reshaping tasks beneath the surface.

Underemployment Rate
Annual, Philippines 2005–2025 • Underemployed persons as % of employed persons

High, Declining — But Still a Structural Problem

Underemployment started in the low-20% range and gradually fell toward 11–13% by the mid-2020s. It has always dwarfed unemployment, signaling that job quality, not job availability, is the deeper vulnerability. Any future AI-driven restructuring will interact with pre-existing job-quality issues rather than creating them from scratch.

Takeaway: The 2022–2025 AI-era window is not historically anomalous in any of the four headline indicators. The dominant story is post-COVID macro recovery continuing a pre-existing trajectory.

Gendered Trends in the Post-COVID / AI-Era Window (2021–2026)

Zooming into the monthly series exposes who is actually in the labor force and how the pandemic recovery played out differently by sex.

Labor Force Participation Rate by Sex
Monthly, Philippines Jan 2021 – Mar 2026

A Persistent but Slowly Shrinking Gap

Male LFPR sits consistently between 72–77%, while female LFPR rose from the high-40s/low-50s in early 2021 toward the mid-50s by 2023–2026. Early pairs showed a gap of ~25–27 percentage points; recent months show ~20–22 points. More women are now in the labor market than in the mid-2000s, but parity is still far away.

Unemployment Rate by Sex
Monthly, Philippines Jan 2021 – Mar 2026

Equal Employment Odds Conditional on Participation

Once in the labor force, men and women face broadly similar employment chances. In many months, male and female unemployment rates differ by only 1 percentage point. By 2023–2025, unemployment for both sexes dropped to 3–5%, down from 7–9% in early 2021. The main driver is macro recovery, not any AI-era shock.

Underemployment Rate by Sex
Monthly, Philippines Jan 2021 – Mar 2026

Job Quality: The Real Gender Story

Male underemployment started in the mid-to-high teens (16–21%) while female underemployment was slightly lower (12–17%). Both improved over time, settling near 11–13% for men and 8–12% for women by 2024–2026. Underemployment remains roughly double the corresponding unemployment rate — for both sexes — even when headline employment is near 96–97%.

Key insight: The gender gap in the Philippine labor market is primarily a participation gap, not an employment-quality gap. Once women enter the labor force, their chances of finding (some) work match men's closely — the harder problem is that many fewer women enter in the first place.

Sectoral Employment Trends (2012–2026)

Is the Philippines following the classic structural-transformation path from agriculture toward industry and services? The data show a clear answer.

Employment Shares by Sector
Annual, Philippines 2012–2026 • Agriculture, Industry, Services

A Service-Dominated Labor Market

Three clear movements stand out:

AGR

Agriculture: Steady Decline

From ~32% in 2012 to ~18% by 2025–2026. Still a significant employer but no longer the dominant one.

IND

Industry: Modest, Stabilised Growth

Rose from ~15–16% to ~18–19% in the mid-2010s, then plateaued near 18% — a supporting, not leading, role.

SVC

Services: The New Center of Gravity

Rose from ~53% in 2012 to ~64% by 2025–2026, absorbing departing agricultural workers and dominating the labor market.

Why this matters for AI: By the time generative AI began diffusing globally in the early 2020s, a growing majority of Filipino workers were already in services — precisely the sector global research identifies as most exposed to AI-enabled automation and task restructuring (BPO, clerical work, trade, finance). The Philippines thus enters the AI era with its center of gravity already in the highest-exposure zone.

The AI-Era Window: Continuity, Not Rupture

The most important empirical question is whether generative AI's mainstream arrival after late 2022 coincides with a visible break in Philippine labor-market trends. The answer, at the aggregate level, is no — yet.

Monthly Employment Rate with Generative-AI Era Marker
Philippines Jan 2021 – Mar 2026 • Shaded band = post-late-2022 AI window

Trends Continue Rather Than Break

When a vertical band is placed at late 2022 to mark the AI-era window, employment was already improving before generative AI became mainstream. There is no sharp jump or collapse exactly at that inflection point. The improvement in unemployment — from ~7–9% in early 2021 to ~3–5% by 2023–2025 — follows the macro recovery from COVID-19, not any new AI-specific shock.

Three Enduring Realities That Shape AI's Impact

Participation Gap
Women's LFPR still ~20 pp below men's. AI changes will hit a labor force that is growing but not yet equal in gender composition.
Service Dominance
64% of workers are in services — the highest-AI-exposure sector. Restructuring here has outsized labor-market consequences.
Underemployment
~11–13% underemployment even in 2025. AI could upgrade these jobs or deepen precarious, low-hour arrangements.
The dual-risk framing: For the Philippines, the AI-era risk is not primarily unemployment (headline numbers are healthy) but rather (1) deepening underemployment if AI reduces hours and task complexity in services without raising wages, and (2) widening gender gaps if AI displaces service roles disproportionately occupied by women who are only recently entering the labor force.

Final Findings & Stylized Facts

The analysis yields five core stylized facts that together characterize the Philippine labor market as it enters the AI era:

01

Headline indicators have improved

Employment is high (~96%), unemployment has trended from ~8% to ~4–5%, and the economy has recovered from COVID-19. No AI-era macro shock is visible in national aggregates as of early 2026.

02

Underemployment is the deeper problem

Structurally elevated at 11–13% even in 2025 (vs. ~4–5% unemployment), underemployment reveals that job quality — not job availability — is the persistent vulnerability. AI will land on this pre-existing weakness.

03

Gender gaps are large but slowly narrowing

Female LFPR rose from ~49% (pre-pandemic) to ~54% (AI era); female labor force share from 39% to 42%. The gap is real but converging. Service-sector growth has been the primary vehicle for women's increasing attachment to the labor force.

04

Structural shift toward AI-exposed services is complete

Agriculture fell from 32% to 18% of employment (2012–2026); services rose from 53% to 64%. The Philippines enters the AI era with its labor-market center of gravity in the highest-exposure sector.

05

AI's impact is potential, not yet visible in macro data

The 2022–2025 window shows continuity, not breaks. The current data form a baseline for future AI-era analysis — not proof of transformation. Evidence-based, cautious policy is warranted.

Bottom line for policy: Whether AI becomes a tool for upgrading work or a force that deepens existing inequalities depends on three pre-existing conditions this study has documented: persistent participation gaps, service-sector dominance, and high underemployment. Addressing these structural weaknesses is the most actionable AI-era labor-market policy in the Philippines today.
